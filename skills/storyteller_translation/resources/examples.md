# Storyteller — Good/Bad Output Examples

## Example: Paid Ads return problem (orders.csv)

### BAD output (DO NOT produce this)

Problems: confidence-clause constructions, causal overreach, rescaled percentages, report tone.

> **Paid Ads Return Rate Analysis**
>
> The data suggests that our Paid Ads channel performed significantly worse than other channels in terms of return rates. We're quite certain that the 57% return rate for Paid Ads, compared to 0% for Organic Search and 25% for Social Media, represents a statistically meaningful difference. This is likely the core reason our profits are flat.
>
> Out of every 100 paid ad customers, approximately 57 would have returned their orders. CUST-1001 was responsible for a portion of these returns.

---

### GOOD output (target voice)

Correct: spoken cadence, raw counts, entity concentration upfront, no causal overreach, one light analogy.

> Honestly? The ads brought in orders, but a lot of them came back. 4 of the 7 orders from paid ads got returned, so yeah, the revenue looked decent on paper, but about half of what those ads generated didn't actually stick.
>
> And it's a bit like counting people through the door at an event without checking how many left before it ended — the headline looks bigger than what you actually kept.
>
> Here's the thing though. A lot of that return problem is basically one person. Same customer, three separate orders through paid ads, all Electronics, all sent back — $405 worth. So that 4-out-of-7 return rate? Three of those four are the same account.
>
> People who came through regular search didn't return anything. Neither did most of the social media orders. Small numbers, so you can't call it a rule. But the direction is there.
>
> What we can't answer yet is whether the ads actually hurt profits. That needs the ad spend and the product costs, and neither were in the data. So this is probably a piece of the story. To close the loop: pull those two numbers, and someone should look at that one customer's account.

---

## What makes the good version work

1. **Entity concentration in the same breath**: "$405 worth, three of those four are the same account" — immediately after stating the 4-of-7 headline
2. **One analogy, used once**: "counting people through the door... without checking how many left" — matches the data shape (inflated headline), then dropped
3. **Causal claim blocked**: "probably a piece of the story" not "this is why profits are flat" — ad spend and COGS absent from data
4. **Raw counts**: "4 of the 7" not "57%" or "57 out of every 100" — n=15
5. **Uneven rhythm**: sentences stop abruptly, restart, vary in length — actual speech, not composed prose
6. **No section headers**: one continuous block
